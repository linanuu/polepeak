4 Beginner Pole Spins to Try in Your First Month

By Lina 7 min read

Before inversions, before flow, before any of it — every poler starts with spins. Fireman, Chair, Front Hook, and Back Hook are the four moves you'll see in nearly every beginner class, and for good reason: they teach your body the fundamentals of grip, momentum, and contact points that everything else on the pole builds on. Here's exactly where your hands go, how to generate spin without flinging yourself off balance, and which parts of your body actually do the holding.

Before you spin: the two things that matter more than strength

New polers usually assume spins are all about arm strength. They're not — a lot of it comes down to contact points (which parts of your skin are actually gripping the pole) and momentum (how you generate the swing before you commit your weight). Get those two things right and the spin gets noticeably easier to hold. That said, everyone builds strength and confidence at a different pace — don't be discouraged if a move that clicks for someone else in class takes you longer.

1. Fireman Spin

The one to learn first

The Fireman Spin stays low to the ground and doesn't ask for much momentum, which makes it the safest first spin to try. It's also the best move for simply feeling what "wrapped around the pole" is supposed to feel like.

Hand placement

High and crossed

Stand facing the pole. Reach your inside hand high overhead in a regular true grip. Bring your outside hand across your chest to grip the pole as well, palm facing you.

Momentum

Step, then swing

Step your inside foot forward and let your outside leg swing out in a wide arc around your body. The wider the swing, the more spin you'll carry into the hold — no yanking with your arms required.

Body contact

Both legs wrap in

Hook your outside leg around the outside of the pole, ankle crossed to the opposite side. At the same time, bring your inside foot up and press it against the back of the pole, ankle crossed the same way.

Keep your arms strong and resist the urge to let your chest collapse toward the pole as your feet come up — that's what causes an unwanted fast spin on a spinning pole.

2. Chair Spin

The one that teaches push-pull

Chair Spin looks effortless in videos, but it's really where you learn the arm pattern — push with one arm, pull with the other — that shows up in almost every pole trick from here on.

Hand placement

Half-bracket grip

Stand beside the pole and place both hands on it in a regular true grip — one high, one lower — rising onto your tiptoes as you engage your shoulders.

Momentum

Swing wide, then tuck

Step your inside foot and swing your outside leg in a wide arc for momentum. As your inside foot leaves the ground, bring both legs up into a tucked, seated position.

Body contact

Arms do the holding

Unlike the other three spins here, your legs don't hook the pole at all — your seated position is held entirely by grip and the push-pull of your arms.

Push away from the pole with your bottom arm while pulling up and in with your top arm. Never let your shoulder go loose or "hang" — keep it pulled down and back the whole way through.

3. Front Hook Spin

The one that unlocks other moves

Front Hook is one of the most useful spins you'll ever learn, since the hand placement carries directly into transitions and tricks at every level after this.

Hand placement

High hand, chest hand

Inside hand high above your head. Outside hand reaches across your chest to grip the pole, palm facing in, thumb up.

Momentum

Push off the standing leg

Rise onto your tiptoes, hook your inside leg around the front of the pole, then use your outside leg to push off and start the spin.

Body contact

Knee pit does the work

Your inside knee pit sits against the pole with the heel tucked back toward your booty. Once you're spinning, bring your feet together to meet in a diamond shape.

If your knee pit is sticking and you can't get moving, the angle is usually too square-on to the pole — tuck the heel in further so the kneepit meets the pole at a slight angle instead of dead center.

4. Back Hook Spin

The one that needs commitment

Back Hook is the mirror image of Front Hook, but because you're generating momentum by falling backward instead of forward, it usually takes beginners a session or two longer to feel confident committing to it.

Momentum

Lead with the leg, fall back

Walk your feet slightly away from the pole for room to extend. Rise onto your tiptoes, lift your outside leg up high, and let your body fall backward, leading with that leg.

Body contact

Knee pit meets the pole

As you fall back, your inside knee pit meets the pole. Bend both knees so your toes come together and you settle into the hold.

The most common mistake here is not lifting the leg high enough before falling back — a low, timid leg lift almost never generates enough momentum to complete the spin.

FAQ

How long does it actually take to learn these four spins?

Most beginners get a wobbly but complete version of the Fireman Spin in their very first class, since it stays low and close to the ground. Chair, Front Hook, and Back Hook usually click within the first one to two weeks of regular practice. Clean, controlled versions on both sides can take a few months — that second side always lags behind.

Do I need to wear shorts for these spins?

For Fireman, Front Hook, and Back Hook, yes. Each of these relies on bare skin at the knee pit or thigh gripping the pole, and fabric there will just slide. Chair Spin is more hand-and-arm driven, so it's the most forgiving of the four if you're still building up to bare-leg moves.

Why do I keep sliding down the pole mid-spin?

Almost always a grip issue, not a strength issue. Check that your top hand is high enough that your arm is close to fully extended, and that you're actually using the push-pull pattern — pushing away with the lower arm while pulling in with the upper one. A cold or freshly-cleaned pole can also feel slicker than usual; warming it up with a dry cloth helps.

Should I use grip aid as a total beginner?

Most experienced instructors suggest holding off for the first few months if you can. Reaching for grip aid too early means your hands never build their own natural stick, and you end up depending on the bottle. It's fine to use a small amount on a specific move you're drilling, but let your grip strength develop on the easier spins first.

Is it normal to get bruises learning these spins?

Yes — a few bruises on the inner knee and thigh are a normal part of the first few weeks as your skin and soft tissue adjust to holding your weight against the pole. They should fade and become less frequent as your technique improves and you rely less on brute contact and more on correct positioning. Sharp or worsening pain is different from ordinary bruising and is worth mentioning to your instructor.

Which of these four spins should I learn first?

Fireman Spin first — it stays close to the ground, doesn't require much momentum, and teaches you how your body feels wrapped around the pole with minimal risk. Chair Spin is a natural second step since it introduces the push-pull grip pattern you'll reuse everywhere. Front Hook and Back Hook build on both of those.
