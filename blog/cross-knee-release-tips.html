How to Get Your Cross-Knee Release: Tips to Stop Sliding and Trust the Layback

June 18, 2026 6 min read By Lina Beginner+ to Intermediate

The cross-knee release is one of those moves that looks impossible and feels worse the first time you try it — leaning all the way back off a single hooked knee, hands floating free, head toward the floor. Here's the secret almost nobody believes until they're in it: the position is far more secure than your brain is telling you. The real block is rarely strength. It's trust. So let's build both, in order.

What the cross-knee release actually is

From a secure pole sit, you hook one knee across the pole, lock in your contact, drive the free leg up, and slowly lower your upper body back until you can release your hands. Your grip in the finished shape isn't your hands and it isn't a death-squeeze of your thighs — it's the hooked knee plus the skin contact behind it. Get that contact right and the rest of the move stops fighting you. It's a Beginner+ to Intermediate skill, and the one true prerequisite is a confident, stable pole sit — your layback is only ever as safe as the seat it starts from.

The grip is in the lock, not the squeeze

The most common beginner mistake is trying to glue both legs together into one solid block to "grip" the pole. It overcomplicates everything and it doesn't actually make you secure. The two legs have different jobs. One knee crosses and hooks. Then — and this is the part that makes it click — you pike up slightly to press the back of that knee and your inner thigh into full contact with the pole, and only then straighten and drive the other leg. Cross, lock, drive. Not clamp.

Start in a secure pole sit

Sit tall and settled before you think about leaning anywhere.

Why: a stable base removes the wobble before you commit. If the sit isn't solid, fix that first.

The fear is in the descent, not the shape

Here's what every poler who has this move will tell you: the frightening part is the lean back. Once you're actually laid out in the position, it feels far more secure than the journey into it. That gap — between how scary the descent feels and how stable the end shape is — is exactly where people get stuck. Your body is ready before your nervous system believes it.

So build the trust in layers. Trail a hand underneath you for support as you learn, and lower just a few inches at a time instead of committing all at once. Some polers find that training in shoes quiets the mental block — the little extra weight makes the lean feel more grounded. And know this from the start: bailing is a skill, not a failure. If something feels off, place a hand down and come out. The confidence to let go comes precisely from knowing you can stop. This is as much a head game as a body one — I wrote more about that in the solo training mindset.

Train it safely

Before you commit to this one: learn it with a spotter and a crash mat, and get it securely a few times in class before you ever try it alone. Coming back up out of the layback is genuinely core-heavy, so have someone nearby who can help with the return early on — and condition your core off the pole so the up-phase is reliable, not a gamble.

Above all, don't rush it. Listen to your body, and let this be a move you earn over weeks, not one you force in a session — that's how the scary ones become the secure ones.

Pole laybacks and inversions carry a real risk of falling. Learn this move under qualified instruction with a spotter and a crash mat, train within your limits, and bail any time something feels off — especially when practising without a spotter.
