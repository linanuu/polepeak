Grip Strength 101: Exercises to Stop Slipping and Start Holding

June 17, 2026 7 min read By Lina

If your hands keep sliding off the pole right when a move is about to work, you are not bad at pole. You just haven't built the grip yet — and grip is built, not born. This is the exact stuff I drilled to go from white-knuckling a basic spin to actually holding it.

First: not all slipping is weak grip

Worth getting this straight before you blame your forearms, because it changes what you fix. Slipping comes from two different places:

Your grip gives out. The muscles in your hands and forearms fatigue, your hold loosens, and you peel off. This is the one you train away, and it's what the rest of this post is about.

Friction fails. Sweaty palms, lotioned skin, a dusty pole, cold hands — your grip might be plenty strong, but there's nothing for it to bite into. No amount of forearm work fixes a slippery surface.

So before a big session: wipe the pole down, skip the moisturiser on training days, warm your hands up, and if your skin is naturally sweaty, a grip aid is a tool, not cheating. Sort the friction first — then the strength work below is what takes you from "held on" to "owned it."

The kind of grip pole actually uses

You don't need a crusher's handshake. Pole leans on two qualities most: supporting grip (holding your bodyweight off a bar or pole without your hand opening) and endurance (keeping that hold for the length of a combo, not just a split second). A bit of pinch strength helps too, for the grips where your thumb does real work. The exercises below build all three, and none of them need a gym — most need nothing but you and something to hang from.

Test your baseline first

Before you train grip, it helps to know where it sits today — and it's genuinely fun to watch the number climb week to week. Hang from a pull-up bar (or hold a firm two-hand grip on your pole, feet down, leaning your weight into your hands) and start the timer. Stop the second your grip starts to give.

The exercises that build it

Pick three or four to start. You don't need all of them, and you don't need fancy kit — bags of shopping, water bottles, and a sturdy bar cover most of it.

Dead hangs

The single best return on effort for pole grip. Just hang from a bar with straight arms and hold. It trains the exact supporting grip and endurance you use on the pole, and it decompresses your shoulders as a bonus.

3–5 hangs · hold as long as good form lasts · rest 90s

Active & towel hangs

Once a plain hang feels easy, loop a towel over the bar and hang from the ends. The thicker, less stable grip forces your hands to work far harder — and it carries over beautifully to gripping a pole.

3 hangs · one towel per hand · build the time slowly

Pinch holds

Pinch a thick book, two stacked plates, or a full water bottle by the lid between fingers and thumb, and hold it out in front of you. This is the thumb-and-finger strength behind the grips where your thumb wraps the pole.

4 holds · 20–30 seconds each side

Farmer's carries

Grab something heavy in each hand — kettlebells, loaded shopping bags, water containers — and walk. Simple, brutal, and it builds the grip endurance that keeps your hands closed through a long combo.

3–4 walks · 30–40 seconds · heavy but controlled

Wrist curls & reverse wrist curls

Rest your forearm on your thigh, hold a light weight, and curl the wrist up — then flip your hand over and do the reverse. These directly target the forearm muscles that close and stabilise your grip.

2–3 sets · 12–15 reps each direction

Band finger extensions

The one almost everyone skips. Loop a rubber band around your fingertips and open your hand against it. All the exercises above train closing; this trains opening, which keeps the two sides balanced and protects you from the achy forearms and elbow niggles that overworked grip can bring on.

2 sets · 15–20 slow opens · both hands

How to actually program it

  • Two to three times a week is plenty. Grip recovers fast, but tendons don't — pile on more and you invite the niggles, not faster gains.
  • Add a little each week. Five more seconds on a hang, one more carry, a slightly heavier bottle. Tiny, steady overload is what makes grip grow.
  • Stack it onto pole days. A few hangs at the end of a session, after you're warm, is the easiest way to stay consistent without adding a whole new workout.
  • Respect the ache. Forearm tightness is normal; sharp wrist or elbow pain is not. Back off and let it settle if something twinges.
